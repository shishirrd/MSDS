Conclusions

Essay Question 1) (5 points) Based solely on these data, what are plausible statistical reasons that explain the failure of the original study? Consider to what extent physical measurements may be used for age prediction.

Answer: There are some plausible statistical reasons as to why the study failed. I believe it is due to an overly simple approach for predicting age and classifying life stages based only on physical measurements like number of rings or volume. While these features do show a relationship to the abalone’s age, particularly in their early life, they become less reliable as the abalones become older. For example, young abalones with lower weight and volume tend to have fewer rings, indicating that there is some correlation between size and age in early development. But this relationship does not consider sex as a factor, when our boxplots represent that relationship very well (see 5b, where the plots showed that females tend to start heavier and larger than males and other infants). The data does not classify infants by sex and this could be one reason why infant abalones have significant variability in their early stages that physical measurements alone can account for. The age-predictive potential of physical measurements declines further as the abalones go into CLASSES A3 through A5. The plots in 5c show that weight and volume stop correlating with the number of rings, suggesting that abalones reach a plateau in their physical growth at some point. This further supports the hypothesis that size and age are not consistently predicting their age and using physical measurements as the sole predictors becomes highly unreliable. To conclude, the study’s inconsistencies in accounting for categorical factors like infant sex or reproductive maturity, likely contributed to its failure. Predicting their age accurately might require more features and a biologically grounded framework , rather than trying to infer age from size alone.

Answer: If I was presented with only an overall histogram and summary statistics from a sample, and no other context, I would ask myself and others several questions before accepting them as representative of the broader population. (1) How was the sample collected? Was it random or convenience-based? (2) What is the sample size as a percentage of the population? (3) What is the hypothesis being tested and why are we testing it? Essay Question 3) (3 points) Do not refer to the abalone data or study. What do you see as difficulties analyzing data derived from observational studies? Can causality be determined? What might be learned from such studies?

Answer: In my experience, analyzing data derived from observational studies presents several challenges, primarily due to the lack of a controlled environment where observations may not be random or may have bias. This makes it difficult to isolate the impact of a specific variable or even variables influencing each other. As a result, causality is generally not definitive in observational studies. The best we can hope for is to find correlations within the data and explore causality as a secondary step. However, this is not to say that observational studies are useless. They are helpful in identifying patterns and generating a hypothesis to be studied and tested further. These hypotheses can be expanded into a study over longer periods of time or extrapolation to a larger population in low-information scenarios. For example, the impact of wearing a mask in stopping the spread of COVID-19 was untested in the strictest sense. However, observational studies showed that certain groups who wore masks more often spread COVID-19 less and the downside of mandating masks was low for most governments.
